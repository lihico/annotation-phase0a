0 What You Are Evaluating

Therapist Responsiveness
Definition. Therapist responsiveness is the capacity to adapt interventions to the patient's evolving state in real time (Stiles et al., 1998). It integrates three steps: reading the patient's current state, acting in a way that fits that state, and monitoring whether the action helped. Responsive therapy means doing the right thing at the right moment for this specific patient.
What responsiveness IS:
  • Contingency: the therapist's action is a response to the patient's current state, not to a pre-set agenda. This is the strongest component (An et al., 2025: contingent adjustment explained 52% of outcome variance, vs. 24% for variability alone).
  • Flexibility: the therapist adjusts when the patient shifts. Note: continuing the same approach when the patient is deepening IS flexible.
  • Benefit: the patient moves forward, stays flat, or moves backward after the intervention.
What responsiveness is NOT:
  • Not intervention quality. A beautifully delivered interpretation is high quality. If the patient needed containment, not reframing, that same interpretation is non-responsive. Quality evaluates the intervention in isolation; responsiveness evaluates it in context.
  • Not variability. A therapist who uses many different techniques is flexible, but if those techniques are not matched to the patient's state, this is not responsiveness.
  • Not empathy alone. Empathy is perceiving the patient's state. Responsiveness requires the additional step of acting appropriately on what was perceived.
The key question for every therapist move: Given what the patient was presenting at this moment, was this the right thing to do?
Example of the distinction: A therapist delivers a skillful meaning-making interpretation linking the patient's relational volatility to her attachment history. The patient responds with a flat "mm-hmm" and shifts topic. The interpretation is high quality (well-formed, clinically grounded) but non-responsive: the patient was signaling a need for support and validation, not reframing. The same interpretation at a different moment, when the patient is actively exploring her patterns, would be both high quality AND responsive.
Rating scales and ground rules
Contingency (1-5): 1=completely mismatched to patient state, 3=relevant but imprecise, 5=precisely targeted to the patient's emerging edge.
Flexibility (1-5): 1=identical repetition despite patient change, 3=moderate shift, 5=creative level-change or appropriate continuation.
Benefit (1-5): 1=clear backward movement (patient closes), 3=flat/neutral, 5=clear forward (new material, deepening).
Opportunity Match: Matched (intervention well-fitted to patient's state and readiness), Partial (partially matched), Mismatched (intervention did not match patient's state or readiness), No clear opp. (no signal or opportunity present).
Direction: Supporting (validates, deepens, stays within patient's frame) vs. Challenging (introduces new frame, pushes toward unexplored territory).

Ground rules: Ground claims in transcript and/or clinical summaries. Patient self-state movement is the primary reference point. Flag uncertainty explicitly. Distinguish content (what was discussed) from process (how it was held).
